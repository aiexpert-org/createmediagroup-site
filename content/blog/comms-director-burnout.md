There's a number I've been tracking with friends who pastor mid-sized churches. The median tenure of a church communications director, in my admittedly anecdotal sample, is somewhere around 18 to 24 months.

That's the median. Some are longer. A lot are shorter. The role chews through young, talented people and spits them out into corporate marketing jobs that pay 40% more and don't require them to be at the office on Sundays.

I want to talk about why — because I think most senior pastors believe the problem is hours, and the problem is not hours.

## The problem isn't hours. It's surface area.

A comms director at a 600-attendance church is usually responsible for, in some combination:

- Sermon series art and slides
- Weekly announcement graphics for 5-12 different ministries
- The church's main Instagram + Facebook
- Each ministry's separate social channels (sometimes)
- The bulletin
- The website
- Email newsletters
- Event campaigns (camp, conferences, baptism services, Easter, Christmas)
- Sermon recording / livestream coordination
- Hospitality signage, lobby screens
- Whatever the senior pastor texted at 9 PM Wednesday
- Whatever the kids pastor texted at 7 AM Thursday

That's not one job. That's four jobs. And the corporate marketing role they're competing with is *one* job. Even if the corporate salary were the same, the corporate role would still win on the surface-area-of-decisions axis.

## What actually breaks them

The hours are bad. The hours are not what breaks them. What breaks them is:

1. **No one else on staff can do any of it.** They can't take a real vacation. Every week they don't make the slides, the slides don't get made.
2. **They're the youngest person on staff.** They're being asked to manage volunteer designers, push back on senior leadership, and make brand decisions, while being 24 and three years out of college.
3. **They're being judged on Sunday morning, every week.** A failed product launch in corporate marketing means a follow-up meeting. A weird-looking slide in church means 40 people glance up at the wrong moment.
4. **Their workload swings violently with no warning.** A normal week is 35 hours. The week before Easter is 80. They can't plan a life around it.

You can't fix the third and fourth ones structurally — they're inherent to the role. But the first two? Those are absolutely fixable.

## What helps (and what doesn't)

**What doesn't help:**

- "Take a Sabbath." (They will, and then double-shift Monday to catch up.)
- Hiring a part-time admin. (Doesn't solve the design surface area.)
- Buying them a better laptop. (They don't need a laptop.)
- Reorganizing the org chart. (Doesn't change the work.)

**What actually helps:**

- **Take design off their plate.** Outsource it to a real designer who specifically does church work. The comms director gets to be a strategist, not a slide-monkey.
- **Give them backup that doesn't depend on volunteers.** Volunteers vanish in summer. Volunteers can't be the load-bearing wall.
- **Centralize their request system.** One inbox. One place where ministry leaders submit needs. Not seven different ministry leaders texting them at 9 PM.
- **Set a hard limit on what they own.** It's okay if the kids ministry has its own designer separate from the comms team. It's okay if the website is owned by someone else. Comms doesn't have to mean everything-visual.

This is what the design subscription model exists for. Not because the church can't afford a comms director — they can. But because the comms director's job, as currently scoped, is structurally impossible. Carving out the design layer and handing it to an outside designer makes their job possible again.

## The 18-month clock

If you have a comms director right now, set a calendar reminder for the 18-month mark. That's roughly when they'll start interviewing.

The fix isn't to ask them to work less. It's to make their job something a person can actually do.
